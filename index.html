Skate Setup Calculator

With all the options out there finding the perfect skate setup can be a daunting task. Every part has it advantages and disadvantages, but what do you choose? This calculator is to help you evaluate some of the skate setup combinations.

One thing to keep in mind is that a good skate setup doesn’t make a good skater. At the end of the day a good skater will adapt to their setup and be able to perform on just about anything. It does help a skater to developer better and faster when using a setup that fits their needs.

Boot Top

Skate Setup Impact: Minor

Affects: Stability, Agility

Low Top: (+Agility) Low top boots are generally considered speed boots. They are more popular and allow a higher range of ankle mobility, and it will also not be as hot on your foot.

High Top: (+Stability) High tops generally offer more ankle support that adds to skater stability, so if you have weaker ankles; high tops would be a good choice.

Boot Heel

Skate Setup Impact: Medium

Affects: Acceleration, Agility, Speed

Raised Heel: (+Agility) Large heels help put more weight toward the front of the plate. This helps with agility and control. Also, with most art boots, there is additional ankle / lower shin support to help with control. The trade off is that the added control kills top speed. As you start to lean over to fight the g-forces, the plates get forced over on their edges. When the plates are forced on the edges hard enough they will want to turn harder than needed. To keep the plates from edging too hard, the skater has to keep their legs upright and without much bend. This causes the stick-up-the-butt form that figure skaters have when they are trying to get up speed. It is an awkward skating position, it requires lots of skill and muscle to get any speed. The steps end up choppy and robot like.

Flat Heel: (+Acceleration, +Speed) A flat or low heel offers the ability to lean over without forcing the plates onto an edge. Lower heels allows a more even weight distribution on the front and back of the plate, especially when the skater is low and leaned over. This allows the weels to stay in contact with the floor longer and the longer the wheels stay in contact with the floor, the more power the skater can put down, and faster they will go. The tradeoff with low heel speed boots is the loss of agility. The edges are controlled more by the ball of the foot and lower part of the ankle rather than the ankle and lower shin.

Plate King Pin Angle

Skate Setup Impact: Major

Affects: Stability, Agility, Speed

Smaller Angle (10°): (+Stability, +Speed) Plates with a smaller king pin angle are more stable and create a more upright skating stance. This offers higher stability especially at higher speeds. On the down side this also requires more force to turn and manurver, sacrificing agility.

Larger Angle (45°): (+Agility) Plates with larger king pin angles offer the opposite of their smaller angled counterparts. Larger angled plates offer more agility and a lower skating stance. They take a larger level to skill and practice to get good with. Overall larger angle plates tend to less percise and controlled.

Plate Mounting

Skate Setup Impact: Major

Affects: Stability, Agility, Acceleration

Standard: (+Stability, +Acceleration) Standard mounting is the most common. It grants a good all around balance for stability and acceleration.

Short-Forward: (+Agility) Short forward means you buy a plate at his shorter than recomended and mount the fron axel under the ball of your foot. This configuration grants you a lot of agilty and responsiveness and as a bonus the skates are lighter, as well. The purpose of forward mounting is to emphasize ‘heel toe’ skating. Technically, there are substantial advantages to being able to reach with your back outside wheel, then power all the way through to your front inside wheel as you complete your push. The downfall of this setup is it reduces stability on impacts, and requires greater skill for explosive acceleration.

Plate Cushion Hardness

Skate Setup Impact: Medium

Affects: Stability, Agility

Hard (100A): (+Stability) Harder cushions tend to be used to compensate for other aspects of a skaters setup. Cushions are a cheap way to make a 45° plate less responsive while the skater builds the skills to contoll it. In general harder cushions reduce the ability for the trucks to flex, reducing agility and increasing stability.

Soft (78A): (+Agility) The opposite can be said about softer cushions. This grants the trucks a greater ability to flex around the king pin. This makes for sharper edging and increased agility.

Wheel Diameter

Skate Setup Impact: Medium

Affects: Acceleration, Speed, Stability

Small (45mm): (+Acceleration, +Stability) Smaller diameter wheels allow for faster acceleration because they take less effort to get you moving. A smaller diameter wheel has less distance to move to get a full revolution than a larger diameter wheel. Smaller diameter wheels on average are more stable than larger diameter wheels. With less distance between you and the ground, it’s easy to see why a smaller diameter wheel would give you more stability.

Large (70mm): (+Speed) A larger or taller diameter wheel will accelerate more slowly and take more effort to get moving. A larger diameter wheel typically has a better overall roll time and can achieve top speeds over a smaller diameter wheel. A larger diameter takes more effort to get moving, but once it does get rolling, it takes less effort to keep it moving fast. This is one reason why long distance speed skaters prefer taller wheels because after they get the wheel moving, they don’t have to exert as much effort. You will also see that most taller wheels are made for outdoor use.

Wheel Width

Skate Setup Impact: Minor

Affects: Agility, Speed, Stability

Narrow (31mm): (+Speed, +Agility) The contact patch is the area of the wheel that is in contact with the surface you are skating on. The more narrow contact patch wheels have less stability, are lighter and make it easier to make quick movements.

Wide (44mm): (+Stability) A wider contact patch equals more grip and more stability. However, it is also heavier, slower and harder to make quick movements on. A wider wheel also adds to the sidways stability of the skate setup.

Wheel Durometer

Skate Setup Impact: Major

Affects: Agility, Speed, Stability

Soft (78A): (+Agility, +Stability) With a softer wheel, you get more grip and a much softer ride – perfect for small pebbles and the normal bumpiness of an outdoor surface. Softer wheels can also be used indoors, too, if you are on a slippery surface and need more grip. Softer wheels are also better for the beginner because they provide more grip. This grip also makes it easier to latteral movement such juking. The softness comes with some major downfalls though, they require substatnially more effor to keep rolling and even more to maintain speed.

Hard (103A): (+Speed) Harder wheels are usually better for tighter, indoor, coated surfaces as these wheels provide less grip. Harder wheels are great for more speed and give more of a slidey feel to the wheel even when on tight floors. This is usually very advantageous to the more advanced skater as it gives you the ability to go faster with less effort. Very hard wheels are also used in artistic skating as they allow the skater to spin more freely on a tight, indoor surface.
